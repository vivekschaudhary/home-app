# Compass — Canonical Framework References

Reference material cited by Compass workflows' "Framework grounding" sections. Each entry: **name · originator · year · one-sentence contribution.** Reference material, not an essay — additions go here in the same shape.

Cited via short-form in workflows: e.g., `[working-backwards]` → links to that entry.

## Strategy / discovery foundations

### working-backwards
**Working Backwards / PR-FAQ** — Amazon (Jeff Bezos, ~2004); popularized in *Working Backwards* (Bryar & Carr, 2021). Start from the customer outcome (a draft press release + FAQ) and derive the product backward — forces the team to write the win before designing the build.

### lean-mvp
**Lean Startup MVP + Build–Measure–Learn** — Eric Ries, *The Lean Startup* (2011). Treat product features as falsifiable hypotheses; validate the smallest experiment that produces signal before scaling.

### continuous-discovery
**Continuous Discovery Habits** — Teresa Torres, *Continuous Discovery Habits* (2021). Weekly customer contact + opportunity-solution trees as the cadence for keeping the product-discovery loop alive (vs. quarterly research rounds).

### jtbd
**Jobs-to-be-Done** — Clayton Christensen (formalized ~2003; lineage to Theodore Levitt 1960). Frame features by the job the user hires the product to do, not by the user's demographic; "people don't buy quarter-inch drills, they buy quarter-inch holes."

## Competitive position

### porter-5-forces
**Porter's Five Forces** — Michael Porter, *Competitive Strategy* (1980). Industry-level analysis of supplier power, buyer power, threat of new entrants, threat of substitutes, and rivalry; tells you whether the industry is profitable to enter.

### helmer-7-powers
**Helmer's 7 Powers** — Hamilton Helmer, *7 Powers* (2016). Company-level moat analysis: scale economies, network effects, counter-positioning, switching costs, branding, cornered resource, process power. Compass extends to a 9-type catalog for foundational-product defensibility.

### blue-ocean
**Blue Ocean Strategy** — W. Chan Kim & Renée Mauborgne, *Blue Ocean Strategy* (2005). Create uncontested market space rather than competing in red oceans of head-to-head competition; framework includes the "four actions" (raise / reduce / eliminate / create).

## Bet-based commitment

### shape-up
**Shape Up — "shape → bet → build" cycle** — Ryan Singer / Basecamp, *Shape Up* (2019). Six-week appetite-bounded cycles; work is shaped before it's committed; the bet table replaces backlogs.

### helmer-bet-portfolio
**Helmer's mathematical bet portfolio model** — Hamilton Helmer / Deep Strategy investment firm methodology. Frames strategic decisions as a portfolio of bets sized by 7 Powers' expected value; informs how Compass treats foundational + feature bets as a measurable wager set.

## Communication discipline

### pyramid-principle
**Pyramid Principle / TL;DR-at-top** — Barbara Minto (McKinsey lineage, *The Minto Pyramid Principle*, 1973+ editions). Conclusion first, supporting arguments next, evidence last — the inverse of how thinking happens, but how reading happens.

### stripe-2-page
**Stripe's 2-page narrative tradition** — Stripe internal practice (~2014+, surfaced in engineering blog posts and Patrick Collison interviews). Cascading strategy docs at fixed page budgets force forcing-function compression; budget = signal of seriousness.

### amazon-6-page
**Amazon's 6-page narrative memo** — Amazon (Jeff Bezos, ~2004). Meetings open with silent reading of a 6-page narrative memo; PowerPoint banned. Forces the writer to do the synthesis the reader would otherwise have to do live.

## Goal-setting

### okrs
**OKRs (Objectives & Key Results)** — Andy Grove (Intel, 1970s) → John Doerr, *Measure What Matters* (2018). Objective = ambitious qualitative goal; Key Results = measurable outcomes that prove the objective. Compass uses for foundational + quarterly OKR bets.

### north-star
**North Star Framework** — Amplitude (Sean Ellis lineage, formalized ~2017-2019). Single metric that captures core customer value; serves as the strategic compass for cross-functional alignment.

## Architecture frameworks

### well-architected
**AWS Well-Architected Framework** — Amazon Web Services (2015; sustainability pillar added 2021). Six pillars: **reliability · security · performance efficiency · cost optimization · operational excellence · sustainability**. Compass uses the six pillars as scoring axes for every foundational architecture decision (each stack pick gets per-pillar rationale). Framework is cloud-vendor-named but principles apply to any deployment target (on-prem, multi-cloud, edge).

### evolutionary-architecture
**Building Evolutionary Architectures** — Neal Ford, Rebecca Parsons, Patrick Kua (2017; 2nd edition 2022). Introduces *fitness functions* as continuous architectural tests — objective, measurable criteria the architecture must satisfy. Compass operationalizes via the "derive fitness functions before stack choices" ordering in `/setup-foundation-architecture`: fitness functions name the falsification criteria; stack picks derive from them.

## Compass-original patterns

### cite-or-mark-na
**Cite-or-mark-n/a enforcement** — Compass principle #15. For N-category structured consultations (Researcher 6-category, Architect 6-pillar + 6-research, Architect signal-consultation 5-category, Story standard-experience 6, Architect playbook 5th), each category produces a citation OR explicit `n/a — <reason>`. Empty cells and unjustified n/a both fail.

### refuse-escalate
**Refuse + escalate to upstream artifact** — Compass principle #16. When a workflow detects an attempt to silently widen an upstream decision (foundational stack, data model, fitness functions), it refuses and escalates to the workflow that owns the decision. No silent in-place widening.

### soft-spec-hardening
**Soft spec → AI rationalization → hardening recipe** — Compass principle #14 (foundational). Anywhere an agent has interpretive room, it exercises judgment that diverges from intent. The fix is explicit constraint + mechanical verification gate + named anti-pattern in the workflow file. Drives the gate/work/postcondition template structure.

### elicitation-with-options
**Elicitation-with-options** — Compass-original interaction pattern (v0.3.2). When a workflow must surface choices to the user (stack picks, configuration decisions, posture declarations), it presents **3 widely-used options + an "Other (specify)" escape valve**. The first decision is **static** (an anchor — same 3 options regardless of context); subsequent decisions **cascade** (options biased by prior picks for coherent combinations). Each pick is captured with cited option + rationale in the workflow's output artifact. Reduces agent rationalization (principle #14) at decision points where genuine user input matters — replaces "draft with smart defaults, ask user to approve" with "ask user, present curated options, capture pick." First applied in `/setup-foundation-architecture` (4 cascading stack-layer elicitations after an anchor decision).

### freshness-check
**Freshness-check** — Compass-original pattern (v0.3.3). Compass docs that reference external-tool formats / APIs / conventions get **frontmatter markers**: `last_verified: <date>`, `freshness_window_days: <N>`, `external_source: <URL>`. Workflows that depend on those docs add a **Precondition step** that reads the markers and refuses if `today - last_verified > freshness_window_days`. Refusal points the user at the canonical external source + the Compass file to update. Missing `last_verified` is treated as infinitely stale (forces one-time backfill on first use). Closes the soft-spec-rationalization surface where Compass docs silently go stale against evolving external tools (principle #14: "the docs might be current, let me try anyway"). **Pull-bridge round 1** of three: v0.3.3 = workflow-side date check (this); **round 2 — shipped v0.3.7** (after 3 consecutive slips from v0.3.4 → v0.3.5+ → v0.3.6+): framework-side detection via `compass/scripts/check-freshness.py` + `.github/workflows/freshness-check.yml` — script walks `compass/` for files with `last_verified:` frontmatter; queries external sources (GitHub API releases/tags/commits or HTTP Last-Modified); auto-bumps `last_verified` where the source is unchanged (safe); flags where it has changed (manual review); errors when network fails. Workflow runs Mondays 06:00 UTC; opens PR for bumps or Issue for flags-only weeks. **Honest accuracy bounds:** detection is HTTP-level (timestamp comparison), not semantic — a doc page may change cosmetically without affecting Compass; the script flags it anyway. Auto-bump only happens when external is UNCHANGED (safer to flag than silently mark stale docs fresh). **Round 3 (v0.4+):** distribution — Compass framework updates auto-propagate to consuming repos as PRs (still deferred; multi-consumer reality observed in v0.3.7 cycle — aura-app at v0.2.x + crypto-app at v0.3.x with no sync mechanism between them — strengthens the case). First applied: Codex review format in `/build` (refusal when `compass/roles/reviewer.md` is stale). **v0.3.6 extension — review-time application:** the pattern also applies at review time, not just at doc-load time. When a story or DRI Decision names a runtime behavior or file convention as load-bearing ("Next.js middleware uses X", "Vercel Functions support Y in this region"), Codex re-verifies the claim against the named tool's current primary docs rather than trusting the story-as-written. Story claims about framework behavior can go stale between when written and when reviewed. See `compass/roles/reviewer.md` "Review-time freshness" subsection.

### role-boundary
**Role-boundary** — Compass-original pattern (v0.3.4). Workflow steps that load or transition roles include structured HTML-comment markers: `<!-- COMPASS_ROLE_BOUNDARY: <enter|exit> | role=<name> | workflow=<id> | step=<N> -->`. The markers serve two purposes: **documentation** (translators / readers see explicit role transitions) and **parser anchors** (the reference parser at `compass/scripts/token-usage.py` maps Claude Code session messages to workflow steps using these markers, then attributes tokens per role). Compass ships the protocol + a sample Python 3 stdlib parser; consumers can adopt as-is or fork. **Accuracy is rough, not exact** — the parser assumes Claude executes steps in linear order; user interruptions, multi-message steps, and out-of-order tool calls complicate attribution. Report includes a Confidence footer naming the heuristics used. PM-owned by convention (matches existing `/status` + `/plan` ownership of "make work visible" jobs). First applied: `/build` (Engineer + Codex + Tech Writer markers across Phase 1–7).

### agent-handoff
**Agent-handoff** — Compass-original pattern (v0.3.5). When a workflow routes work between AI agents (Engineer → Reviewer, Architect → Engineer, etc.), Compass defines a 5-piece handoff shape so the user is not the bridge: **trigger artifact** (the medium that signals "next agent's turn" — PR, issue, label, file) · **trigger event** (what fires the next agent — GitHub event, webhook, status check) · **context window** (what state the receiving agent reads) · **output medium** (where receiving agent posts response) · **loop signal** (how the sending agent knows there's a response to react to). Compass ships a reference GitHub Actions template at `compass/scripts/agent-handoff.yml` parameterized over the reviewer agent — the consuming repo copies it to `.github/workflows/`, picks ONE reviewer block (Codex / Claude headless / Gemini / generic), sets the matching API-key secret, and the Engineer → Reviewer handoff stops requiring tool-switching. **Agent-agnostic by design** — the pattern abstracts over which AI plays the reviewer role. First applied: `/build` Phase 5 step 13 references the automated path (with manual fallback). Vendor CLI install commands and flags are **drift-prone** — sibling README tracks `last_verified` per `[freshness-check]`.

### agent-agnostic-role-assignment
**Agent-agnostic-role-assignment** — Compass-original pattern (v0.3.8). When the framework assigns AI agents to roles, the assignment is **config-driven against a declared agent registry**, not hardcoded across prose. `compass/config.yaml` ships three blocks: **`agents:`** (registry declaring each supported agent's invocation pattern — `cli` / `api` / `manual` — context-loading convention — `local-files` / `api-system-prompt` / `manual-paste` — and auth requirements); **`defaults:`** (sensible defaults matching the framework's empirically-validated split — Claude implements, Codex reviews); **`tool_assignments:`** (per-role agent picks, validated against the registry, falling back to defaults). Supported agents at codification: **claude** + **codex** (production today, local-file context loading); **openai** (ChatGPT / GPT API — API mature, Custom GPT discoverable, manual paste degraded); **gemini** (Google Gemini CLI — mirrors codex with `.gemini/prompts/`); **deepseek** (DeepSeek API); **codestral** (Mistral API); **apple** (marked `unsupported: true` — Apple Intelligence is system-level without an open API for arbitrary role-playing; documented honestly rather than faked); **custom** (escape valve for user-defined agents). **Why config-driven, not hardcoded:** today (pre-v0.3.8) `tool_assignments` is documentation only — nothing programmatically reads it, and 10 files independently hardcode the Claude+Codex split in prose (`AGENTS.md`, `README.md`, `CLAUDE.md`, `SETUP.md`, `.claude/skills/build/SKILL.md`, `.codex/prompts/reviewer.md`, `.codex/prompts/security-reviewer.md`, `compass/framework/canon.md`, `compass/workflows/build.md`, `compass/workflows/fix.md`). v0.3.8 introduces the registry as source-of-truth; AGENTS.md "Tool division of labor" reframes from hardcoded prose to "default + override against registry"; downstream prose-derivation propagation is deferred to **v0.3.9** (`compass/scripts/setup-agent.py` propagation script that generates per-agent prompt directories for CLI agents — `.codex/prompts/`, `.gemini/prompts/` — from `tool_assignments`). **Adapter layer is upstream, not Compass-side** (v0.3.8 same-day correction) — API-based agents (openai, deepseek, codestral) use existing adapter libraries (LiteLLM recommended; Vercel AI SDK / OpenRouter / LangChain alternatives). Compass declares the registry; adapter routes the API calls. No per-agent adapter docs ship with Compass — duplicates upstream documentation. **L2 (per-agent adapter docs) is explicitly NOT a Compass deliverable.** **Two instances at codification:** (1) `[agent-handoff]` v0.3.5 — agent-agnostic for one role (reviewer) via the GitHub Actions template's 4 commented reviewer blocks (Codex / Claude headless / Gemini / generic); (2) v0.3.8 — agent-agnostic for all roles via the config-driven registry, generalizing the same shape from reviewer-only to every role. **2nd handoff-class member** (joining `[agent-handoff]`); catalog balance becomes **4 enforcement : 5 usability** (interaction · freshness · observability · 2 handoff). **Defaults work out of the box** — if a user changes nothing, behavior matches pre-v0.3.8: Claude implements, Codex reviews. Migration is opt-in (one-line `tool_assignments` edit per role). **Forward-compatible with the orchestrator vision (v0.4+):** the `agents:` registry shape naturally extends to declare an `orchestrator` entry; per-workflow-agent declarations follow the same shape. v0.3.8 ships the foundation that the v0.4 / v0.5 multi-agent layer extends rather than replaces.

### mechanical-output-verification
**Mechanical-output-verification** — Compass-original pattern (v0.3.6). When a workflow requires a build, deploy, or framework-discovery step, the **postcondition is inspection of the build OUTPUT or runtime artifact, not just the build PROCESS exit code**. Source intent and build output can diverge silently — the build process can complete cleanly while the runtime configuration drops what the source declared. **Inspect the artifact that actually runs**, not the source that was supposed to compile into it. Sharper version of Principle #14 — the soft spec being rationalized is increasingly subtle ("the build succeeded" / "the tests pass" / "the principle is cited in the comment"); the fix is reading the actual runtime artifact, not trusting surrounding signals. Framework-specific anchors: **Next.js 16** (`.next/server/functions-config-manifest.json` — `/_middleware` entry registers middleware/proxy on Next 16+; `routes-manifest.json`, `app-paths-manifest.json`, `prerender-manifest.json`); **Pre-v16 Next** (legacy `.next/server/middleware-manifest.json` — empty by design in 16.x; cross-check with functions-config-manifest.json on 16+); **Vercel Functions** (`.vercel/output/functions/`); **Expo** (prebuild native config + bundle); **general principle** — when runtime configuration is data-driven (manifests, bundle indexes, config JSON written by the build), reading source ≠ reading runtime. **Two instances at codification:** (1) `/build` Phase 2 step 7 build-artifact inspection from aura-app CB-1.4 dashboard proxy (middleware-manifest.json check revealed gap between source intent and runtime config, 2026-06-01); (2) Codex's own retrospective from the same cycle ("Start with framework registration checks before reading functional tests" + "Prefer 'is this actually deployed by the framework?' over 'do the tests pass?'"). Closes the **`polished-but-broken`** anti-pattern (tests pass + build green + narrative coherent + behavior wrong). Applied: `/build` Phase 2 step 7 (Engineer self-check) + `compass/roles/reviewer.md` Step 0 (Codex review process — framework-registration check before functional analysis). **4th enforcement-class member** (joining cite-or-mark-n/a · refuse-escalate · soft-spec-hardening); resets catalog balance to 4 enforcement : 4 usability.

### declare-not-implement
**Declare-not-implement** — Compass-original pattern (v0.3.9). When a workflow needs an integration with external tools, agents, or services, **Compass declares the pattern + registry + manual fallback; it does NOT write the integration itself.** Upstream libraries, vendor CLIs, or consumer-side wiring handle the actual integration. Compass identifies the integration surface, declares the structural shape (pattern name + registry entry + reference template or documentation pointer), and **refuses to build implementations that would duplicate upstream/community work**. **First scope-discipline class Compass-original — introduces the 6th pattern shape.** Catalog now spans 6 shapes across 10 patterns: enforcement (4) · interaction (1) · freshness (1) · observability (1) · handoff (2) · **scope-discipline (1 — this)**. Scope-discipline patterns govern what Compass declares vs builds vs delegates — meta-discipline about the framework's own scope, distinct from the workflow-level shapes that govern how workflows execute. **Why this works as discipline:** integrations decay (vendor APIs change, CLIs evolve, libraries fork). Compass-maintained integrations would (a) duplicate documentation that upstream maintainers already publish, (b) go stale as upstream evolves, (c) put Compass in a maintenance role that's not its job. Declaring the pattern + registry (the structural surface) is durable; building the integration is fragile. The pattern is honest about which work Compass takes on and which work it delegates. **Two instances at codification:** (1) **v0.3.5 `[agent-handoff]`** — Compass declared the 5-piece handoff shape (trigger artifact · trigger event · context window · output medium · loop signal) and shipped `compass/scripts/agent-handoff.yml` with **4 commented reviewer blocks** (Codex / Claude headless / Gemini / generic CLI). Each block names the install command + invocation flags as **best-effort references for the consumer to verify**. Compass did not write or maintain per-CLI integrations; the consuming repo customizes per their reviewer agent. (2) **v0.3.8 same-day correction (`[agent-agnostic-role-assignment]` adapter layer)** — Compass declared the `agents:` registry with `invocation` / `context_loading` / `auth_env` fields per agent. For API-based agents (openai, deepseek, codestral), Compass **explicitly delegates** to upstream adapter libraries (LiteLLM, Vercel AI SDK, OpenRouter, LangChain). The L2 work that would have written per-agent adapter docs at `compass/agents/<agent>.md` was **caught and removed as scope creep** within an hour of v0.3.8 substance shipping. User reality-check: *"we are not creating per agent adapter we will use an existing adapter like litellm and other competitors for the adapters."* **Anti-pattern it closes: `integration-creep`.** When integration surfaces expand (new AI provider, new vendor CLI, new framework version, new MCP server), the temptation is to add per-integration documentation or adapters to Compass. **Integration scope grows linearly with the integration count; Compass-maintainer scope does not.** The result is stale Compass docs, brittle Compass adapters, and a framework whose maintenance burden grows past its sustainable size. `[declare-not-implement]` is the structural countermeasure — invoke when about to ship integration work that should be upstream or consumer-side. **Applied at framework design time, not workflow execution time.** Unlike the 5 workflow-level shapes (enforcement / interaction / freshness / observability / handoff), this pattern doesn't fire during a workflow run. It fires when Compass's own scope is being decided — typically during release planning, codification, or roadmap deferrals. **Load-bearing oversight is the user.** v0.3.8's same-day correction was caught by the user, not by the framework alone. Without user reality-check, Compass would have shipped the 8 per-agent adapter docs as L2 — the exact scope creep this pattern names. The framework's `[declare-not-implement]` reflex is real but not infallible; user judgment is part of the system. **Forward-compatible with future Compass-originals:** any future pattern that ships a registry + reference template + upstream delegation declaration follows the same shape. Likely future candidates: orchestrator selection (v0.4+) declares orchestrator runtime registry + delegates to Claude Agent SDK / OpenAI Assistants / LangGraph; consumer distribution (v0.4+) declares sync protocol + delegates to user-driven pull mechanism. Each would be additional `scope-discipline` members.

### agent-as-surface-independent-unit
**Agent-as-surface-independent-unit** — Compass-original pattern (v0.3.14). When Compass assigns AI work, the unit of assignment is the **agent file** (`compass/agents/<agent>.md`), which is **self-sufficient + surface-independent**: a single markdown file containing identity + inlined principles + required-and-optional tools + task definitions (gate/work/postcondition) + refusal rules + handoffs + framework-knowledge references. The same agent file works on any LLM host — ChatGPT Custom GPT Instructions, Claude session, Codex prompt, Gemini system message, CrewAI agent definition, LangGraph node. **Host is an LLM runtime, not a role authority.** Workflows become **thin dispatch graphs** — ordered sequences of `<agent>.<task>` references — with workflow-level invariants (preconditions, HITL gates, verification) declared at the workflow layer. The methodology IP that used to live in workflow step bodies moves into agent task definitions; workflows declare the sequence. **First architecture-discipline class Compass-original — introduces the 7th pattern shape.** Catalog: 7 shapes / 12 patterns: enforcement (4) · interaction (1) · freshness (1) · observability (1) · handoff (2) · scope-discipline (2) · **architecture-discipline (1 — this)**. Architecture-discipline shape governs the **structural composition** of agents / workflows / hosts — distinct from scope-discipline (what Compass declares vs delegates) and workflow-execution shapes (how individual workflows enforce gates). **Anti-pattern named: `host-coupled-role-definition`.** When role definitions are coupled to a specific LLM host via wrapper files (CLAUDE.md owning role authority, would-be CHATGPT.md as analog, .codex/prompts as another host wrapper), the role definition fragments across N host wrappers as new hosts are added; updating discipline means editing N files; portability requires cross-translation. Same-shape, multi-file-drift problem. **The structural countermeasure:** consolidate the role + task content into one file per agent; declare host preference as an agent property (`preferred_hosts:` in the agent file's frontmatter); make every host (CLAUDE.md, CHATGPT.md analog, CODEX.md analog) thin runtime-notes-only — no role authority. **Three instances at codification:** (1) **config.yaml `tool_assignments:` audit (2026-06-04).** User-driven audit confirmed `tool_assignments:` is pure declaration — nothing programmatically reads it; 10 files independently hardcode the Claude+Codex split in prose. Changing `tool_assignments.pm: claude → openai` has zero behavioral effect. The same audit was foreshadowed in `[agent-agnostic-role-assignment]` v0.3.8 ("today (pre-v0.3.8) tool_assignments is documentation only — nothing programmatically reads it") and named explicitly in `improvements.md`. Reaffirmed in 2026-06-04 cross-host-shift conversation. (2) **CHATGPT.md proposal died on its own logic.** When user described shifting PM / Researcher / UX to ChatGPT for a new project, the natural extrapolation was to ship a CHATGPT.md file analogous to CLAUDE.md. The proposal collapsed in design — Custom GPT Instructions aren't auto-loaded from a repo file (the load-bearing copy is what the user pastes into the Builder); CHATGPT.md would either duplicate Instructions content (drift surface) or be ignored. Same pattern would re-emerge for every new host. The right answer was NOT a CHATGPT.md but **dissolving host-files entirely** — agent files own the role authority, host files become thin runtime-notes. (3) **v0.4 multi-host orchestration vision (v0.3.12 spec target).** The Delivery Manager + Time/Quality/Finance + cross-host dispatch architecture requires agent files as the unit the orchestrator dispatches. CrewAI / LangGraph / AutoGen agent definitions are the upstream shape; Compass agent files are the source-of-truth that the orchestrator (when shipped) consumes. v0.3.14 ships the shape; v0.4 ships the orchestrator. **The agent file is structured as:** YAML frontmatter (`name`, `preferred_hosts:`, `required_tools:`, `optional_tools:`, `participates_in_workflows:`, `version:`) → Identity → Core principles (INLINED — must hold without external file load; discipline cannot be a remote dependency) → Tasks owned (gate/work/postcondition per task; inputs/outputs/handoffs declared explicitly) → Refusal rules → Framework knowledge (REFERENCED — fetch from `compass/framework/canon.md` if host has access; operate with degraded depth otherwise and tell the user explicitly) → Output summary contract → Anti-patterns → Host capability degradation table (per missing tool: what discipline applies as compensation). **Hybrid inlining principle (v0.3.14 design choice):** what's load-bearing-on-every-host (discipline principles, task gates, refusal rules) is INLINED in the agent file; what's deep-but-stable (full canon entries, source guides, framework references) is REFERENCED. Trade-off: agent file is larger than pure-reference but smaller than full-inline; works without external file access; degrades gracefully when external access is missing. **Workflow-level vs task-level content separation:** workflow files own (a) cross-agent preconditions, (b) dispatch graph (ordered `<agent>.<task>` sequence), (c) HITL gate placement, (d) workflow-level verification (cross-agent invariants). Agent task files own (a) per-step gate/work/postcondition, (b) task-specific inputs/outputs, (c) task-specific handoffs (upstream/downstream agent references), (d) per-task refusal rules. **The mental test:** if a constraint applies across two or more agents in the workflow, it's workflow-level. If it applies within one agent's task, it's task-level. **What this pattern kills:** CLAUDE.md / CHATGPT.md / CODEX.md owning role authority (they become thin runtime-notes); `config.yaml.tool_assignments:` as routing config (deprecated; per-agent `preferred_hosts:` replaces it; user keeps `tool_assignments:` during v0.3.x grace period as override, deleted in v0.4); per-host role definition duplication (single agent file is the canonical source). **What this enables:** cross-host orchestration without host-wrapper proliferation; CrewAI / LangGraph / AutoGen orchestrator drops in naturally; adding a new agent = write one file (no CLAUDE.md edits, no config.yaml routing); forking agents (Senior PM vs Junior PM, domain-specialized Researcher) = copy + edit; surface-independent paste-into-any-host operation. **Forward-compatible with v0.4 orchestrator:** orchestrator reads workflow dispatch graph, looks up agent's `preferred_hosts:`, dispatches via the appropriate host's API. Agent file contents become the system prompt; task-step inputs become the user prompt. **Migration path:** v0.3.14 ships 3 agents (pm, researcher, engineer) + 1 refactored workflow (/setup-product) as the minimum to unblock new-project kickoff. Other 10 agents migrate as new project needs surface (v0.3.15+); other 13 workflows refactor as their owning agents migrate. `compass/roles/<role>.md` files kept during v0.3.x grace period as content sources for unmigrated agents — removed in v0.4 once all agents migrated. **Forward link to candidates surfacing:** `[task-ownership-locality]` (one task = one owner agent — currently 1 instance, codify after 2nd) and `[workflow-as-dispatch-graph]` (workflows are thin dispatch contracts, not methodology bodies — currently 1 instance, codify after 2nd). Both surfaced during v0.3.14 design conversation; await 2nd instance per Compass codification rule. **Distinct from `[agent-agnostic-role-assignment]` (v0.3.8):** v0.3.8 declared a registry of WHICH agent plays each role (config-driven assignment); v0.3.14 redefines WHAT an agent IS (a self-sufficient surface-independent unit) such that the assignment becomes a property of the agent file itself, not a separate registry. v0.3.8's `tool_assignments:` becomes the legacy override layer; v0.3.14's `preferred_hosts:` (per agent) is the new source-of-truth. Generalizes from "registry of choices" to "agents own their host preference; registry becomes optional override".

### fractal-retro
**Fractal-retro** — Compass-original pattern (v0.3.17). When the framework's discipline (periodic pattern review via retros) is single-altitude — only the framework's own improvement log gets retroed — the **bottom-up signal is lost**: patterns visible at the role / workflow / bet / project altitudes have nowhere to surface, and the framework retro averages across cycles too coarse to filter that signal. `[fractal-retro]` is the structural countermeasure: **same retro workflow shape applied recursively at every altitude** (role · workflow · bet · project · org · framework), with lower-altitude retros reading raw activity / run / DRI logs, and higher-altitude retros reading + consolidating child retros via a `consolidates_from:` frontmatter field. The same `compass/templates/retro.md` shape works at every altitude; the `altitude:` and `parent_log:` frontmatter fields specialize each instance. Higher altitudes promote patterns UP via "Promotion candidates to <parent altitude>" sections; canon promotion happens when a pattern rises three altitudes (role → project → framework, or workflow → project → framework). **2nd architecture-discipline class member** (joining `[agent-as-surface-independent-unit]` v0.3.14). **Catalog: 7 shapes / 13 patterns:** enforcement (4) · interaction (1) · freshness (1) · observability (1) · handoff (2) · scope-discipline (2) · **architecture-discipline (2 — `[agent-as-surface-independent-unit]` + this)**. ### user-as-load-bearing-oversight

### hard-line-declaration
**Hard-line-declaration** — Compass-original pattern (v0.3.10). When Compass commits to shipping something in a specific future release (a substantive deferral — "freshness detection v0.3.4+", "Retro #005 after improvement #25"), the commitment gets **explicit slip-counters + named consequences** in CHANGELOG entries and `compass/workflows/improvements.md` headers. The visibility + named consequence creates **structural pressure** that overcomes the diffuse "next substantive release is more important than this commitment" rationalization. Without the hard line, the commitment drifts indefinitely (v0.3.4 → v0.3.5+ → v0.3.6+ → never); each slip is individually defensible, but the cumulative pattern is rationalization — Principle #14 applied to roadmap. **The pattern is mechanical** in three parts: **(1) Counter visibility** — track slip count in a load-bearing place (CHANGELOG release notes, improvements.md header next-retro counter). **(2) Named consequence** — declare what happens at N+1 ("if it slips a 4th time, the v0.3.3 workflow-side defense must be re-examined"; "if retro slips again, retro rationalization is no longer one-off; elevate `[hard-line-declaration]` codification or tighten slip-counter visibility"). **(3) Structural pressure** — the explicit visibility + named consequence is itself the enforcement mechanism. Soft commitments to future-self get rationalized away unless structurally hardened — same shape as #14's structural-not-aspirational fix applied to roadmap commitments. **2nd scope-discipline class member** (joining `[declare-not-implement]` v0.3.9). **Catalog grows from 6 shapes / 10 patterns → 6 shapes / 11 patterns** — workflow-execution shapes still total 9 (enforcement 4 · interaction 1 · freshness 1 · observability 1 · handoff 2); scope-discipline class grows from 1 → 2 members (`[declare-not-implement]`, `[hard-line-declaration]`). **Scope-discipline shape is now structurally validated — not a one-off introduced in v0.3.9.** Ratio: 9 workflow-execution : 2 scope-discipline. **Two instances at codification:** (1) **Freshness detection 3-slip → v0.3.7 ship.** v0.3.3 committed freshness detection to v0.3.4; v0.3.4 bumped to v0.3.5+; v0.3.5 bumped to v0.3.6+; **v0.3.6 CHANGELOG declared hard line:** "if it slips a 4th time, the workflow-side defense from v0.3.3 must be re-examined to confirm it's still sufficient." v0.3.7 shipped freshness detection ON TIME. The hard line created structural pressure that overcame rationalization toward higher-leverage substantive releases. (2) **Retro cadence 2-slip → Retro #005 on time.** Retro #003 fired backfilled (14 improvements covered). Retro #004 fired 2 cycles overdue at improvement #22 instead of promised #20. **Retro #004 declared hard line:** "if retro slips again, retro rationalization is no longer one-off; treat it as recurring soft-spec failure and apply structural countermeasure." Retro #005 fired ON TIME at improvement #25. The hard line worked again — same shape, different domain (retro cadence vs roadmap commitment). **Anti-pattern named: `commitment-drift`.** When Compass commits to a future release and lets it slip silently, the commitment drifts indefinitely. Each individual slip is defensible ("substantive work is more important than this commitment"); the cumulative pattern is rationalization. `[hard-line-declaration]` is the structural countermeasure: name the consequence explicitly, in a load-bearing visible place (CHANGELOG release notes, improvements.md counter), so the next slip can't be silent. **Applied at framework design time, not workflow execution time** — same as `[declare-not-implement]`. Different scope (within-workflow vs across-releases); same family (structural enforcement of soft constraints). **First in-canon instance of pattern-induced compliance documented end-to-end** — both instances trace ship-on-time → naming the structural mechanism that delivered. **Worth tracking how often `[hard-line-declaration]` fires successfully in future release-planning sessions.** Each on-time ship after a declared hard line is a successful application; each slip past a declared consequence is data that the pattern needs sharpening (tighter counter visibility, more specific consequences, escalation to enforcement-class).

### agent-file-compression

All three are observation mechanisms for things the framework needs to see but doesn't automate by default. **The compression playbook is mechanical** (replayable across any agent file): **(1) Hybrid-inlining preserved** — INLINED content (Identity, Core principles, Tasks I own with Gate + Work + Postcondition, Refusal rules, Output summary contract, Anti-patterns, Host capability degradation) cannot be deferred to external fetch per `[agent-as-surface-independent-unit]` v0.3.14; REFERENCED content (deep framework-knowledge entries) gets folded into Host-cap-degradation tail as a one-line pattern-name list. **(2) Per-task structural compression** — task bodies restructure to `Gate:` (preconditions, load-bearing) + `Work:` (compressed step sequence) + `Postcondition:` (load-bearing); drop verbose Inputs enumerations (implicit from Work), Handoffs detail (implicit from workflow dispatch), Triggered-by lines. **(3) Inline-separated lists** — Anti-patterns + framework-knowledge tail use `·` or `;` separators instead of bullet lists. **(4) Host-cap-degradation as table → bullets** — 3-column markdown tables compress to bullet lists. **(5) Frontmatter version bumped** stamping the trim. **(6) No behavior change verified** — same identity, task list, gates, postconditions, refusal rules, `preferred_hosts:`, required + optional tools. Non-chatgpt agents (e.g., reviewer.md targets `[codex, gemini]`, engineer.md targets `[claude, codex, gemini]`) get WARN status when they exceed cap but don't block CI — useful intel for future migrations that add chatgpt support without false-positive merge blocks today. Compressed alongside pm in same release; 5 iterations. ### workflow-as-dispatch-graph
**Workflow-as-dispatch-graph** — Compass-original pattern (v0.3.24). When Compass encodes a workflow, the workflow file is a **thin ordered dispatch contract** — a list of `<agent>.<task>` steps with inputs, outputs, and handoffs declared at the step boundary. Methodology IP lives entirely in the agent task files (`Gate:` + `Work:` + `Postcondition:` per task). **3rd architecture-discipline class member** (joining `[agent-as-surface-independent-unit]` v0.3.14 + `[fractal-retro]` v0.3.17). **Catalog grows from 7 shapes / 15 patterns → 7 shapes / 16 patterns:** enforcement (4) · interaction (1) · freshness (1) · observability (3) · handoff (2) · scope-discipline (2) · **architecture-discipline (3 — `[agent-as-surface-independent-unit]` + `[fractal-retro]` + this)**. Architecture-discipline class is now the **largest non-enforcement class** at 3 members; its shape and durability are validated across 3 distinct domains (agent-host composition, retro altitude, workflow structure). **What belongs in the workflow file vs. the agent task file:** workflow-level content = (a) ordered dispatch sequence (`<agent>.<task>` references), (b) cross-agent preconditions and HITL gate placement, (c) workflow-level verification (cross-agent invariants that can't live in a single agent task — e.g., "no open BLOCKERs across all reviewers"), (d) workflow-level patterns (edge cases spanning multiple steps — e.g., "Story → multiple PRs" in `/build`). Task-level content = (a) per-step gate/work/postcondition, (b) task-specific inputs/outputs, (c) task-specific handoffs (upstream/downstream agent references), (d) per-task refusal rules. **The mental test:** if a constraint applies within one agent's step, it's task-level; if it applies across two or more steps (or agents), it's workflow-level. **Anti-pattern named: `embedded-methodology`.** When workflow files own both the sequence AND the methodology (step procedures, discipline principles, quality criteria embedded in prose), drift surfaces multiply: (a) updating a task's methodology requires editing both the agent file AND every workflow that mentions the task inline — two sources of truth; (b) adding a new agent to an existing workflow requires duplicating methodology that already lives in the agent file; (c) cross-host execution is impossible — the methodology is embedded in workflow prose the orchestrator can't parse; (d) different workflow files dispatching the same agent task may embed subtly different methodology, creating divergent behavior depending on which workflow fires. **Why dispatch graphs enable the orchestrator:** a dispatch graph is directly machine-readable. The orchestrator iterates steps, loads the agent file as system prompt, sends task inputs as user message, collects output. Embedded-methodology workflows require a human to interpret prose on every step — they cannot be executed by machine. `[workflow-as-dispatch-graph]` is therefore **the structural prerequisite** for Compass v0.4 orchestrator: without dispatch graphs, the orchestrator has nothing to walk. **Two instances at codification:** (1) **`/setup-product` v0.3.14** — first dispatch-graph workflow. Shipped as part of the `[agent-as-surface-independent-unit]` migration that moved role authority into agent files; `/setup-product` became the reference implementation of the thin dispatch contract shape. (2) **`/build` v0.3.23** — second dispatch-graph workflow. More complex than `/setup-product` (8 steps, 3 agents, HITL gate, dispute branch); confirmed the shape generalizes to multi-agent, multi-step, dispute-handling workflows. The refactor also surfaced a latent dependency: migrating `/build` to dispatch-graph made PM's participation in `/build` explicit (previously implicit in embedded prose → explicit in `pm.md` `participates_in_workflows:` frontmatter). **`[explicit-dispatch-surfaces-latent-participation]` watch-for candidate** (1 instance — codify if pattern recurs in future workflow migrations). **Distinct from `[agent-as-surface-independent-unit]` (v0.3.14):** v0.3.14 defines what an AGENT IS — a self-sufficient surface-independent unit; task definitions (gate/work/postcondition) live in the agent file. v0.3.24 defines what a WORKFLOW IS — a thin dispatch contract; the sequence lives in the workflow file. Together they form the complete composition model: agents own methodology; workflows own sequence. Neither is complete without the other. `[workflow-as-dispatch-graph]` is the complementary constraint that makes `[agent-as-surface-independent-unit]` executable: a workflow that embeds methodology in prose nullifies the surface-independence guarantee (the agent file's task definition becomes irrelevant — the workflow re-defines the task inline, creating drift). **Distinct from `[fractal-retro]` (v0.3.17):** v0.3.17 made RETROS altitude-independent (same workflow at any altitude, altitude as frontmatter). v0.3.24 makes WORKFLOW EXECUTION machine-independent (same dispatch graph parsed by any orchestrator, methodology owned by agent files). Different domain, same structural insight: factor the variable axis (altitude / host runtime) out of the workflow shape and make it a runtime property. **Migration path for the 12 remaining embedded-methodology workflows:** refactor as their owning agents finish migration (complete by v0.4). Priority order per `compass/framework/mvp.md`: `/create-bet-architecture` (Architect migration) + `/create-brief` (Product pack) → `/create-story` → `/triage` → all others. Each refactor is structural-only: same steps, same artifacts, same gates, same refusal cases — just redistribution of methodology prose from workflow body into agent task definitions. **Applied at workflow-design time and at workflow-refactor time.** Unlike `[declare-not-implement]` + `[hard-line-declaration]` (release-planning-time only), `[workflow-as-dispatch-graph]` fires during both new workflow creation and existing workflow migration. New workflows ship as dispatch graphs from the start; embedded-methodology workflows refactor as their agents migrate. **Forward-link to orchestrator:** v0.4 orchestrator reads `<agent>` + `<task>` per step, loads the agent file as system prompt, sends task inputs as user message. The dispatch graph shape is the orchestrator's interface contract — every future agent migration that produces a dispatch-graph workflow adds one more automatable path to the v0.4 execution graph.

### cross-artifact-sweep-on-contract-shift
**Cross-artifact sweep on contract shift** — Compass-original pattern (v0.3.35). When any **contract** changes — API field name or shape, data model field, shared type, environment variable name, exported function signature, component prop interface — **sweep every artifact that references that contract before the PR opens**. Sweep targets: components · API clients · test fixtures · config files · docs · env var declarations (`.env.example`, CI config). Log the sweep as a DRI Decision ("swept N files for contract change X; all updated"). **5th enforcement-class Compass-original** (joining `[cite-or-mark-n/a]` · `[refuse-escalate]` · `[soft-spec-hardening]` · `[mechanical-output-verification]`). **Catalog grows from 7 shapes / 17 patterns → 7 shapes / 18 patterns:** **enforcement (5)** · interaction (1) · freshness (1) · observability (3) · handoff (2) · scope-discipline (3) · architecture-discipline (3). **Anti-pattern named: `contract-change-without-consumer-sweep`.** The build compiles. TypeScript may or may not catch cross-boundary uses depending on how the contract is consumed (e.g., object spread, dynamic key access, JSON deserialization, env var string concat). Runtime consumers that reference the old contract break silently or throw in production with no build-time warning. The failure is invisible until the feature ships — and the fix is "rename it back or sweep again," not a code logic fix. **Why sweep is pre-PR, not review-time:** if stale consumers survive to Reviewer, the Reviewer must grep the full codebase to find them — that's work the Engineer should have done, not extra latency in the review loop. Reviewer's job is code quality, not consumer archaeology. **Five instances at codification** (unusually strong threshold — most Compass-originals codify at 3): (1) **CB-2.2** — API field rename not propagated to all API clients; one client kept the old field name; Reviewer caught it. (2) **CB-2.5** — data model field removed; test fixtures still referenced the removed field; tests passed locally (mocked), broke in CI. (3) **CB-3.1** — shared type made a field required (was optional); not all call sites updated; TypeScript caught some but not the ones under `any` casts. (4) **CB-3.2** — env var renamed (`API_URL` → `PUBLIC_API_URL`); `.env.example` not updated; CI caught it but only after a 10-minute build. (5) **CB-3.3 ×2** — component prop renamed in two separate PRs; consumer components weren't updated in either case; one broke at runtime (RSC boundary — TypeScript can't surface that), one broke in component tests. **Distinct from `[mechanical-output-verification]` (v0.3.6):** MOV inspects BUILD OUTPUT artifacts (manifests, bundles, runtime configs) against the source that was supposed to compile into them. `[cross-artifact-sweep-on-contract-shift]` inspects SOURCE CONSUMERS of a changed contract against the new contract shape — pre-PR, not post-build. Both close failure modes that build success doesn't catch; they operate at different phases (pre-PR vs post-build) and different domains (source graph vs runtime artifact graph). **Mechanics:** (1) identify the changed contract surface (field name, type signature, env key, prop); (2) grep the full codebase for every textual reference (string literal for env vars and field names; symbol reference for types and props); (3) fix or update every hit; (4) re-run typecheck + tests; (5) log DRI Decision with count of files updated. Grep is sufficient for most cases — the discipline is exhaustive search, not static analysis. **Applied pre-PR at implement-story step 7** (per `engineer.md` v0.3.35). Fires when the story's diff includes ANY contract change; skips when the story doesn't touch shared contracts. **Forward-link to candidates:** `[consumer-archaeology-as-reviewer-antipattern]` (if pattern recurs of Reviewer having to grep for stale consumers that Engineer should have swept — 0 explicit instances yet, but implied by CB-2.2 and CB-3.3); `[contract-change-impact-estimate]` (at 3+ instances, sweeping before touching the contract — not after — as an architectural planning step before implementation starts; candidate when that upstream practice recurs).

### per-surface-vertical-test

### discipline-as-muscle-memory
**Discipline-as-muscle-memory** — Compass-original pattern (v0.3.34). When a discipline commitment (retro cadence, pre-push checks, gate enforcement) has fired **on-time for 5+ consecutive instances**, the external enforcement scaffolding established by `[hard-line-declaration]` (counter-visibility, slip-counters, named consequences) can be **reduced** — the habit is structural, not contingent on surveillance. The framework shifts from **enforcement-of-new-behavior** (requires visible pressure) to **maintenance-of-established-habit** (requires only the discipline itself). **Complementary pair with `[hard-line-declaration]` (v0.3.10):** `[hard-line-declaration]` establishes structural pressure for a discipline not yet proven; `[discipline-as-muscle-memory]` is the graduation from that pressure once proven. Together they form a discipline lifecycle: establish (hard-line) → prove (5+ consecutive) → reduce overhead (muscle-memory). **3rd scope-discipline class member** (joining `[declare-not-implement]` v0.3.9 + `[hard-line-declaration]` v0.3.10). **Catalog grows from 7 shapes / 16 patterns → 7 shapes / 17 patterns:** enforcement (4) · interaction (1) · freshness (1) · observability (3) · handoff (2) · **scope-discipline (3 — `[declare-not-implement]` + `[hard-line-declaration]` + this)** · architecture-discipline (3). **Anti-patterns named:** (1) **`premature-scaffold-removal`** — removing enforcement apparatus before the habit is proven (fewer than 5 consecutive on-time executions); each miss has an individually defensible excuse and the cadence collapses back into drift. Threshold is empirical, not arbitrary: 5 consecutive on-time retros in Compass proved the point; fewer would have left ambiguity about whether the cadence was established or lucky. (2) **`scaffold-that-never-retires`** — keeping full enforcement apparatus (slip-counters, named consequences, per-improvement counter-visibility) indefinitely after habit is proven; overhead adds up (longer improvement entries, review cost of counting, risk of "teaching to the test" — optimizing counter visibility rather than the discipline itself). `[discipline-as-muscle-memory]` fires when a scaffold that has served its purpose is overdue to step back. **The threshold (5+ consecutive):** chosen at codification from empirical evidence — Retros #005-#011 represent 7 consecutive on-time retros; PROMOTE to muscle-memory happened at Retro #011 (7th) after PROMOTE recommendation at Retro #010 (6th). The PROMOTE recommendation itself at Retro #010 confirms the threshold is not self-assessed at exactly 5 — the pattern is observed by one retro, executed by the next, giving one cycle for verification. **Single instance at codification (unusual — promoted due to quality of evidence, not count):** Retro cadence — 7 consecutive on-time retros (#005, #006, #007, #008, #009, #010, #011 — improvement-#25, #30, #35, #40, #45, #50, #55 respectively). `[hard-line-declaration]` provided structural pressure from Retro #004 (first overdue retro) forward; by Retro #010 the pressure was clearly structural habit. Retro #011 executed the PROMOTE by reducing counter-visibility in improvement entries (1-sentence vs. multi-sentence cadence tracking). Second instance expected in any future discipline that reaches 5+ consecutive applications — prime candidates: pre-push grep checks (if adopted), agent-cap checks per `[agent-file-compression]` pre-commit (if wired). **Behavioral implication — what reduces (not what disappears):** counter-visibility apparatus reduces (shorter per-improvement cadence reminders); the retro itself, the cadence commitment, the slip-detection in retrospect, and the named consequence for breaking the streak all remain. Muscle memory means less scaffolding, not no scaffolding. The distinction is: enforcement-apparatus (the visible counting + warning machinery) vs the discipline itself (the retro, the gate, the check). The discipline does NOT retire. **Applied at retro-firing time, not framework-design time.** Unlike `[declare-not-implement]` + `[hard-line-declaration]` (release-planning or design-time triggers), `[discipline-as-muscle-memory]` fires when a retro's "Meta" section is written — each retro asks "is this discipline now structural?" and tracks toward the 5-consecutive threshold. The scaffold-reduction decision is made consciously at a retro checkpoint, not silently dropped. **Distinct from `[hard-line-declaration]` (v0.3.10):** hard-line-declaration fires at the START of discipline-establishment (creating structural pressure for a slip-prone or new commitment); discipline-as-muscle-memory fires at the END of the establishment phase (recognizing that structural pressure has become internal cadence). **Forward-link to candidates:** `[scaffold-reactivation]` (if a muscle-memory discipline breaks — misses after scaffold removal — the scaffold reactivates at the same threshold; 0 instances so far; candidate pattern for the day it happens); `[three-altitude-rise-canon-promotion]` (declared in `[fractal-retro]` v0.3.17 for pattern visibility at role + project + framework altitudes; 1 instance, codify after 2nd).

### conditional-dispatch
**Conditional-dispatch** — Compass-original pattern (v0.3.49). A dispatch-graph step can be a **routing gate** whose outcome selects what happens next instead of always falling through to the next step. A route target is one of: an **inline step** in the same graph (forward-only skip of the not-taken branch), a **cross-workflow hand-off** to another workflow (`/fix`, `/create-brief`, `/ops`), or **`close`** (terminal). v1 routing is **HITL-driven** (the agent may propose; the human picks at the gate) and **forward-only** (no loops/backward branches); v1 hand-off **recommends** the next command rather than auto-invoking the child workflow (auto-chaining is deferred to the LLM-driver surface, #87 surface 3). **4th architecture-discipline class member** (joining `[agent-as-surface-independent-unit]` v0.3.14 + `[fractal-retro]` v0.3.17 + `[workflow-as-dispatch-graph]` v0.3.24). **Catalog grows from 7 shapes / 22 patterns → 7 shapes / 23 patterns:** enforcement (8) · interaction (1) · freshness (1) · observability (3) · handoff (2) · scope-discipline (4) · **architecture-discipline (4 — `[agent-as-surface-independent-unit]` + `[fractal-retro]` + `[workflow-as-dispatch-graph]` + this)**. **Anti-pattern named: `linear-graph-routing-dead-end`** — when a workflow can *classify* an item into branches but the executor only walks a single linear sequence, the classification has nowhere to go: the human re-runs the right workflow by hand, or worse, the wrong workflow runs to completion on a misclassified item. Live evidence: home-app `/triage` runs correctly identified a feature-as-bug and a bug-as-incident but had no route to send them down — the dead-end that drove improvement #98. **Why this complements `[workflow-as-dispatch-graph]` (v0.3.24):** that pattern makes a workflow a thin *ordered* dispatch contract the orchestrator can walk; `[conditional-dispatch]` adds *branching* to that contract so the walk is data-driven, not strictly linear — the category→workflow mapping lives in the graph's Routes block (data), never hardcoded in the executor. The engine reads a route target string and acts; it knows nothing about ITIL. **Two instances at codification:** (1) **within-graph fix-forward (#95→#96, v0.3.48)** — `/triage`'s incident branch routes `resolved` straight to postmortem vs `needs-fix` into the fix+review branch; both reconverge. First proof the skip-set executor (`_skip_for_route`) handles inline branching. (2) **cross-workflow ITIL intake router (#98→#103, v0.3.49)** — `/triage` becomes the front door: `support.classify-intake` proposes an ITIL category, the routing gate routes `incident` inline but **hands off** `bug`→`/fix`, `enhancement`/`problem`→`/create-brief`, `change`/`service-request`→`/ops`, and closes `not-an-issue`. This generalized route targets from `int` to `int | str` (the hand-off/close kinds) — the engine change that made cross-workflow branching possible. **Distinct from a plain HITL gate (#70):** an approve/reject gate is binary and promotes-or-halts; a routing gate offers N labeled branches and selects a continuation (or an external hand-off). Both are human-driven; the routing gate adds the branch-selection semantics. **Applied at workflow-design time** (authoring a Routes block) **and execution time** (the orchestrator's routing-gate handler in `run.py`). **Forward-link to candidates:** `[autonomous-routing]` (when the LLM-driver surface lands, the classifier's proposal could auto-route low-risk categories without a human pick — 0 instances; v2); `[handoff-with-context-carry]` (v1 recommends a command; v2 auto-invokes the child workflow with the intake summary as context — the auto-chaining deferred here).
